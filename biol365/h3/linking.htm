LINKING THE PRACTICE OF THE ARTS TO THE STUDY OF SCIENCE:
A DISCUSSION OF METHODS AND MOTIVATIONS

In response to the demands of the job market, our current educational system concentrates on producing graduates with extremely specialized knowledge. The holistic education that was once the hallmark of a liberal arts college has been sacrificed for economic concerns (training for specific jobs). Many educators and employers are coming to realize that students with this type of rigid training often lack the creative thinking and problem-solving skills that they need in a working environment with constant demands to learn and apply new skills. These educators and employers are interested in systems that emphasize active learning as a way to address these concerns.

Many of the authors that explore the origins of creative thought emphasize the role that practicing the arts (rather than simply experiencing them) have in developing well-rounded, adaptive and creative individuals. Historically, the practice of the arts was seen as beneficial to the study of science but our current educational system does not share this view. By recombining the study of science with the practice of the arts, students can realize the benefits of enhanced creativity and better problem solving skills.Our drawing class seeks to incorporate these concepts into the biology curriculum by adding the study of drawing as an aid to improve observational skills.

In a common model of the current biology curriculum, a student is required to memorize and classify a great deal of data. This helps the student begin to understand relationships between various life forms and body parts as well as the systems that enable them to function. In addition to memorization, many instructors require their students to draw the organisms that they are studying, knowing that, through drawing, a different kind of information processing is occurring.

Drawing through observation helps to reinforce the defining characteristics of specific groups of organisms, such as the number of appendages, segmentation, or type of symmetry. Because our educational systems have become compartmentalized, biology and anatomy students are told to draw in order to help them record their observations but they are never given any instruction on how to do this. Without instruction, students who are not predisposed to draw are left feeling incompetent ("I can't draw!") and are unable to take advantage of the benefits this tool offers. Drawing through observation is a skill which most people are capable of learning. Unfortunately, many students are intimidated by the drawing process, having given it up when their inability to draw things realistically caused enough frustration for them to decide that they have no drawing aptitude. Students with this perception of their drawing abilities are unlikely to become enthusiastic when their professor suggests that they draw in order to increase their comprehension. In fact, they complain saying; "This is a biology class, not an art class!" Biology students who take our drawing class learn to use a tool that can be useful to them for the rest of their lives, whether or not they pursue careers in science. The ability to observe the world around them enriches their experience of life, while learning a skill that they may have thought was beyond their reach inspires new confidence in their abilities.

The physical act of drawing mostly consists of developing hand-eye coordination. Anyone who can write legibly has the physical ability to record observations of a subject through drawing. Many people would disagree with this statement by saying that they have no talent for art. Although it is true that there are those with more artistic ability than others, this is hardly a reason to avoid the practice. There are many people of all ages who enjoy the game of basketball even though they will never play at the level of Michael Jordan. Though some people cook better than others, few people eat all of their meals in restaurants. In an academic setting, educators expect their students to reach a certain proficiency in math or written language even though few are capable of the genius of Einstein or Hemingway. For someone studying anatomy or biology, it is not even necessary that their drawing look like the object being drawn. All that is necessary is that the person has processed information through the act of drawing and thereby achieved a new level of understanding. This is the essence of active learning; learning through doing rather than through merely looking at a picture or listening to a lecture. A person can benefit from the activity at any level of proficiency.

The reason that most people have difficulty drawing realistically is not because of any lack of physical skill or talent, but because they have not been trained to really look at what they see. The rationale behind the method we use in our drawing class is based on the one set forth by Betty Edwards in her popular book Drawing on the Right Side of the Brain. The theory behind her work is that we develop and use drawn symbols as a kind of shorthand for sorting visual information. This is because our culture and educational system emphasize the processing of information in the verbal and analytical mode, or what the author terms the L-mode (for left brain). This mode of thinking is efficient because it condenses information into symbols but someone who is over-dependent on it forfeits the capacity for careful observation. A person using this mode will look at an object and quickly arrange it into a simplified assortment of shapes. For instance, when looking at an eye, that person will think to themselves "Oh that's a football shape with a circle inside that has a black dot in the middle of it". Then the person looks at the paper and draws a football shape with a circle and a black dot, never once looking back at the eye to see if the image they created is accurate. A person who is trained to observe, however, doesn't think about labeling the object but will easily spend an hour or more drawing the shapes, the lines and their relationships to each other. This person who, through the act of drawing, truly observes an eye, understands how the lids fit over the eye, how the eyelid has a certain thickness, how the eyelashes grow from the edge of the eyelid, how the pupil is actually an opening into the eyeball, where the tears are formed and how the eye fits into the skeletal and muscular structure of the head. By drawing the eye, the person achieves a much better understanding of the organ than they ever will by memorizing and labeling its parts, a phenomenon noted by the neurologist Santiago Ramon y Cajal, when he stated that "…all great observers are skillful in sketching."

Training a person to observe can be accomplished through a series of exercises that require the student to complete tasks that are extremely difficult to do analytically. For example, students are asked to draw the spaces around objects they see or to copy drawings that are upside down. They have no labels or shorthand symbols for these things and are forced to actually look at what they are drawing in order to put anything down on paper at all. Although these exercises can be quite difficult at first and even can cause headaches, with perseverance they become less difficult as students practice the shift in thinking from an analytical and labeling mode to one that is more intuitive and holistic. This mental change in the method of processing information causes a change of attitude as well. The student no longer experiences feelings of impatience with detail and an anxiousness to "get on with it" but rather feels a sense of curiosity and wonder and is often quite unaware of the passage of time. Often, our students are amazed at the results and are soon quite eager to continue their explorations of the world through the practice of drawing. Many have stated that their experience of the world around them is quite different after they have learned to draw. To be accurate, however, it must be said that drawing a specimen does not replace what the student learns during lectures, homework and readings but rather reinforces and internalizes this information. The two modes of thinking work in conjunction to produce a much deeper understanding than can be gained from either mode in isolation from the other.

Although our drawing class is aimed specifically towards beginning biology students, any discipline that requires the observation of an object or system could take advantage of the benefits that training in drawing offers. The procedures we use in the class can easily be adapted for students at any level of biology, botany and anatomy. Using the same rational we have outlined in this discussion, the University of California, Davis, includes drawing and writing instruction in their fish taxonomy laboratory courses to increase student interest and comprehension. (Description is published in the March/April 1999 issue of The Journal of College Science Teaching.) In addition, there are many other areas that offer the potential to link the practice of the arts to the study of science. The connections between math and music are well documented (during Copernicus' time, the study of music was required for all math students) but there are strong relationships between other areas of science and art as well. The process of throwing a pot is a direct application of physics principles as is using a hammer to shape a piece of metal. Glaze formulation is pure chemistry and designing a comfortable, lightweight yet strong piece of jewelry requires an understanding of both math and engineering. All art students engage in self-examination and self-expression as well as develop awareness of social and cultural issues when working on their individual projects. Furthermore, students in art classes are self-motivated because they choose to make and do things that are interesting to them. Through the making of an object or the performance of music or a play, they practice problem solving and creative thinking skills as well as the systematic application of the concepts they are learning in their other classes. Re-establishing the lost connections between the arts and sciences would benefit many students in many areas of study. In the words of Michele and Robert Root-Bernstein:

"Arts and sciences constantly interact in very fruitful ways that are often overlooked…The arts are not merely for self-expression or entertainment. They are…disciplines as rigorous as medicine or mathematics with their own bodies of knowledge, tools, skills and philosophies. Moreover, because the imaginative tools used in the arts are critical to the humanities and the sciences, they deserve support not just for their own sake but for the sake of education as a whole. Math, science and technology have flourished in the past only when and where the arts have flourished. They will flourish or fail together in the future."
