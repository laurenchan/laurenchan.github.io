Our research focuses on understanding population connectivity in natural systems. We work primarily with amphibians and reptiles and combine field research, genetic data, computer simulations, geospatial data, and computational approaches to answer both basic and applied questions. Below are a few of the recent and ongoing projects.

If you're interested in joining the lab or just learning more about the kinds of questions we ask, drop me an email (lchan at pacificu dot edu).

Conservation genetics of the Dunes Sagebrush Lizard

The Dunes Sagebrush Lizard (left) is endemic to the Mescalero and Monahans Sand-Shinnery Oak ecosystems of southeastern New Mexico and adjacent Texas. With colleagues at Texas A&M and New Mexico Game and Fish, I am studying the evolutionary and more recent anthropogenic factors that influence population connectivity and persistence in this unique landscape. This includes range-wide phylogeographic and fine-scale population genetic studies and the integration of these data with landscape and population ecology datasets. [Read more here]

Evolution of Madagascar's Vertebrates

Madagascar harbors incredible biological diversity and endemicity, much of which is currently threatened or endangered. A large part of my postdoctoral research at Duke worked towards a synthetic understanding of evolutionary diversification across vertebrate groups in Madagascar. We are examining species limits as well as phylogeographic and population genetic structure across a number of endemic taxa including lizards, lemurs, fruit bats, and rodents (see Publications). These phylogenetically diverse datasets provide a unique opportunity to test demographically, geographically, and ecologically explicit hypotheses of evolutionary differentiation. In addition, by combining geospatial modeling and coalescent-based genetic analyses we can estimate the historical demographic parameters surrounding divergence and better evaluate phylogeographic hypotheses (Chan et al. 2011).
